Authored by: Chris Fornesa, Framing by: Claude Sonnet via Perplexity, Edits by: Grammarly.

## The Core Tension

As someone with a lifelong passion for art and creative pursuits (having a certificate in Digital Communication and minoring in Studio Art), but also knowledge of data science and a long-term interest in web development, I have felt myself personally torn between two wants:

* Preserving human creativity and authenticity.  
* Ensuring that humans don't fall behind technological progress due to biases about what is or isn't "real".

What’s clear to me is that generative AI tools are not going anywhere, and the opportunities that creative people, whether they be curious, budding, or professional, now have with these tools at their disposal are undeniable. However, generative AI must be approached cautiously and critically. Simply accepting generative AI outputs without applying critical thought to a decision reflects a mistaken assumption of their correctness and risks cognitive and creative atrophy. Using generative AI as a collaboration tool, in a creative manner that fosters critical thinking, however, can lead to great outcomes, new systems, and the learning of new skills in the process.

## My Reasoning

When I found out about generative AI coding tools in January 2026, namely Claude Code, Codex, and Cursor, I was ecstatic. These tools provided me with ample opportunities to create in ways that I had never been able to do before. Though I already had quite a bit of coding experience (due to graduate-level coursework in software design and an MS in Data Science), I always had the “blank page” issue, which is that I had no idea what to build since, with every decision, you traditionally needed to have programming languages, frameworks, libraries, and functionalities in mind ahead of time. With these generative AI coding tools, aspects of this ideation could now be automated.  
However, I would soon find myself struggling with self-control and increasingly outsourcing my cognitive load in development. As a result, I would lose interest in my projects because I could no longer understand what was going on in the codebase. I realized I needed to learn more about generative AI, and my knowledge of machine learning models and deploying neural networks for my MS capstone project wasn't sufficient. I needed to understand how LLMs specifically worked and to apply these principles to actual projects. So, for a few months, I would take various Coursera programs, even finishing the IBM generative AI Developer certificate and a few “vibe coding” programs, and rekindled my love of software development in the process.   
But one thing was missing: the actual application and deployment. So, in March 2026, I decided to rectify this by building projects again, even though I was intimidated by generative AI coding tools at the time. So I finally decided to challenge this fear, and over time, I developed [my resume website](https://chris.com.ph), a project I had almost abandoned. This was a challenging process, as I had limited experience with Node.js, TypeScript, and Express, and it took extensive trial and error to move from ideation to deployment. And this is what inspired me to develop the Creatrweb framework.  
Immediately after I started using these tools, I noticed that many developers and non-developers alike raised numerous complaints about generative AI coding tools, including moving too fast, cognitive offloading without critical thought, skill atrophy, security concerns, and the like. Given my BA in Liberal Studies, graduate-level coursework in Information Technology, and MS in Data Science, I'm well-equipped to navigate many of these issues. But whereas developers and other tech professionals would continue to emphasize the mid-2010’s “learn to code” ideology, many non-tech professionals were building products with users, regardless of concerns about code quality. So, it dawned on me that the technical skills that really mattered were programming language-agnostic, while it has become necessary to scope out and think about the product that you’re creating. In essence, generative AI coding tools are allowing inexperienced people to try out new ideas and learn new skills by simply prompting and needing to implicitly understand what’s going on in their codebases, which I had failed spectacularly at attempting. But when I finally “vibe coded” an application that functioned well and even included a RAG chatbot, I was hooked.   
So I decided to create a framework to help others like me build real applications creatively and as safely as possible. In my experience, a key issue that kept me from understanding my codebases was my tendency not to ask follow-up or clarifying questions. Simply asking an generative AI coding tool to explain a decision “in plain English” opened doors to my understanding that I had not considered before. At the same time, serious planning work was necessary to ensure that an generative AI coding tool understands your vision, while documenting decisions and memory was also vital to ensure that the tool knows what has or hasn’t worked across disparate sessions and tools. Decision-making (even before building an app) and guidance on guardrails, along with past successes and mistakes, are what the Creatrweb framework did for my process. Combine this with a simple shell script to automate which generative AI coding tool to use, and I was able to seamlessly systemize the “vibe coding” process while legitimately learning, as my tools would now constantly prompt me to make decisions and consider different considerations for crucial ones. In other words, Creatrweb adds the necessary friction to foster genuine creativity without slowing the user down, prompting them to think more critically through aspects of the Socratic method to get to the heart of why they want to build something in the first place. As a result, the resume website that I almost deleted (out of pure spite and frustration) was resurrected as a functioning tool with a design scheme that reflects my personal art style. The lesson here? With necessary built-in friction, intellectual inquiry, and a lot of patience, you can unlock your creativity and engage in active learning using generative AI tools.  
At the same time, the Socratic method (an argumentative tool used to delve deeper into an issue to strengthen an argument) would also frequently come up in content on how to make generative AI usage more fruitful (which personally satisfied me as a Liberal Arts graduate). This was helpful, as the framework was inspired by my exploration of the Indieweb and my initial desire to participate in that community, and by my subsequent disappointment with anti-AI sentiment that seemed more rooted in popular opinion than in intellectual honesty or creative possibility. For instance, I used the Socratic method with Claude Sonnet via Perplexity to generate the first drafts of the markdown that would become the [Creatrweb framework](https://github.com/cfornesa/creatrweb). In the process, I was asked everything from “Why do you believe that the Indieweb is too limited?” to “What is authenticity to you?” With further tuning, I developed this framework for myself in a way that’s [evidence-based](https://www.swansea.ac.uk/press-office/news-events/news/2025/11/can-ai-make-us-more-creative-new-study-reveals-surprising-benefits-of-human-ai-collaboration.php) and questions my own assumptions. In the process, I have also learned much more about the guardrails needed to control generative AI outputs, prompt engineering, and how humans tend to interact with new technologies. At the same time, I sought to help those who are brave and curious enough to challenge themselves to transform their workflows and use generative AI tools in new and different ways to empower their creativity.

## Why Authenticity Is a Loaded Word

Authenticity can be a loaded, if not politicized, notion that is oftentimes obfuscated to fit specific, traditionalist ideas of what is or isn't “really” (insert literally any topic here). In reality, creativity is not limited to explicitly human-only processes. What makes art meaningful is not the composition but, rather, the ideas behind it. The Surrealists were notable not because they intentionally painted in a way that intentionally obfuscates the subject, but rather because they broke out of the realist tradition that Western art had enforced for centuries. Similarly, abstract expressionists like Pollock and their artistic descendants, the neo-expressionists such as Basquiat, broke the rules of form, figure, and representation, while Warhol literally created art he did not craft himself. Yet these were among the most prolific and well-known modern artists of their time. Why? Because they broke the rules, the boundaries, and the public understanding of art at the time.  
So, to me, authenticity is not about restricting the tools and styles you, as the creator, can use or invoke (which is a hallmark of the Indieweb’s general anti-AI sentiment and “Not By generative AI” badges). Authenticity is about the quality of collaboration with the idea itself, as well as with the tools you use, whether you use generative AI to edit your writing or draw an art piece by hand. Similarly, meaning comes from the quality of our interactions in this lifetime, not from avoiding algorithms outright or shaming those who choose to use generative AI in their work. The real question that we should ask in the “age of generative AI” is one of quality, not authenticity. Ultimately, authenticity can only be judged subjectively, given individuals' differing personal contexts, whereas quality is more universal and accessible. So, instead of decrying generative AI-generated content as “AI slop”, try asking how better prompts, context, tools, and models can increase the quality of outputs and how critical thinking can strengthen the quality of the idea(s) behind the output. It’s very simple, and cognitively lazy, to call out something that you don’t like, but it’s more intellectually challenging, yet stimulating and, ultimately, fruitful, to understand how systems work and what meaningful changes are necessary to increase their efficiencies while maintaining human creativity.  
Human creativity is not lost by the tools we use, but it is lost when we begin to cognitively offload at the very moments when ideation is most important. Human creativity can be expressed through generative AI, but only when we choose to embrace creative friction, rather than attempting to smooth out our creative capacities as a whole. Quality is not a simple matter of authenticity but of human creativity and critical thinking, and it also forces authenticity to shine rather than fall into the background.   
In an age of social media algorithms (which employ AI technologies) that, ironically, promote anti-AI sentiment, do not let trends cloud your judgment about the creative potential, legitimate risks, and other issues and benefits of using generative AI. Instead, do your research on what generative AI is capable of doing, the positive use cases possible with generative AI, and how generative AI can legitimately amplify, rather than reduce, your authenticity and unique voice.

## On Generative AI and Creativity

AI and algorithmic harms are very real and are already occurring, but so is algorithmic utility, specifically the creative use cases that artists, programmers, and other creative people have already achieved using large language models. While I have my personal criticisms of the generative AI industry, these criticisms also apply to our economic and legal systems as a whole, rather than being specific to generative AI technology.   
AI technology itself can be used in unethical and immoral ways because humans who thrive on anti-AI sentiment obscure their personal accountability for the harms of generative AI, whether they be job losses, participation in genocidal wars in West Asia and North Africa, or the localized environmental consequences of data centers. However, opposing the technology itself also obscures accountability. It is accepting the status quo of generative AI used to enact state-sanctioned violence, facilitate human job displacement, and is solely powered at the expense of environmental harm, when these systemic issues can (and should) be regulated away. This is our current reality, not the reality or the future that we must accept without question. Regulation should ban the use of generative AI in warfare, demand that generative AI data centers be powered by renewable technologies, fairly compensate local communities for their resource usage, and hold generative AI CEOs and boards of directors liable for unethical practices, such as using loopholes in fair use to train generative AI models using novel human-created works without authors’ permission. But the reality is that we must increase generative AI literacy among the American (and global) population to ensure people understand who should be held accountable and liable for malicious use and training of generative AI.  
However, artists such as [Matt Zien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matt_Zien), the founder of KNGMKR Labs, are using generative AI and other emerging technologies to realize their artistic visions while leveraging their creative expertise. The point here? Generative AI is a new medium that is not yet mature enough to be accepted as an artistic tool, primarily because of narratives around involving generative AI tools in art, rather than the creative expression that’s possible in the process of creating high-quality, meaningful art using generative AI tools, whether as a form of social commentary or to create a beautiful vision. Similarly, anyone with access to a generative AI coding tool (which range from free to $200 Claude Max subscriptions) can create a website that showcases their creativity, so long as they allow themselves to experience the joy and thrill of creative freedom. I have used Google Stitch to successfully redesign several websites, including my resume website. I also designed the website for this framework with an Apple-inspired intent, where the user experiences it as a Mac-inspired desktop in desktop mode and as a Safari-inspired application in tablet and mobile modes. For my resume website, I also consolidated four different applications: the original resume website which only contained a RAG chatbot and personal details, a Tanaga agent to generate poetry with either a Tanaga structure (7-7-7-7 syllable pattern) for Tagalog poems or a Syllabic Verse (8-8-8-8 syllable pattern) for English poems, a Resume agent to tailor a user’s existing resume details and a job description to create a new, tailored resume, and an Art Inspiration agent that integrates open-source art instruction content via retrieval augmented generation to answer user inquiries about various art styles, mediums, concepts, and the like through an art historical and technical perspective. These are complex applications that needed proper management to consolidate, which is why I decided to use Claude Code to create the necessary architecture and Gemini CLI and Codex to refine the details of what is now my resume website. Though I have some design expertise and artists like Matt Zien have other creative expertise, anyone can learn to code, design, and express themselves using generative AI tools by developing the taste and critical thinking necessary to filter the signals of authentic creativity from the noise of hype. In other words, if you want to create, start creating. Don’t wait until you feel ready, especially when you have a vision. This cognitive bias of waiting until you’re ready is a matter of perfectionism-driven procrastination or imposter syndrome, whereas actual learning (especially for creative pursuits like web development or art) only truly occurs by doing. In the same way that an artist cannot develop their skill without making art, a web developer or software engineer cannot develop their skill without making software, regardless of the specific tools they use. So long as you are willing to do, ready to learn, have a vision in mind, and are willing to act in an ethical manner, you are ready to use generative AI tools to create whatever you desire.

## The Real Opportunity

The real opportunity is the ability to apply more, not less, thought into your creative practice. When you use generative AI tools as collaborative tools and sessions as collaborative endeavors, you think differently. You begin to focus on the mission of executing your vision rather than becoming hyper-focused on details such as specific functions, lines of code, specific words, and the like. You also start to see the “bigger picture” more easily and, if done properly, understand how the finer details support it. Understanding the potential benefits, potential risks, and existing use cases for generative AI tools is the first step to taking advantage of this new opportunity. The next step, and the most important, is familiarizing yourself with different tools to understand which you prefer, which you would rather stop using, and the specific ideal use cases for the tools you like. Finally, you can start executing your vision using your preferred tools and methods. This is, of course, where the Creatrweb framework hopefully helps to facilitate your creativity and vision alongside your preferred tools, specifically for generative AI coding tools. By forcing you to define specifications, you are forced to think creatively (by yourself or using generative AI) to define your vision within the confines of the existing documentation in the markdown files associated with this framework, and, in turn, end up with a product or design that better suits your vision.   
While inspired by the decentralized and anti-corporate web ideals of the Indieweb, my hope for the Creatrweb framework also seeks to mesh these ideals with the realities of generative AI tools and to incorporate lessons from several scholarly articles and studies. For example, in June 2025, the [MIT Media Lab](https://www.brainonllm.com/) found that users who manually thought out and used no tools in writing an essay, then were asked to use LLMs (specifically, ChatGPT’s latest model as of Summer 2025\) to write a different essay “demonstrated higher memory recall, and re-engagement of widespread occipito-parietal and prefrontal nodes”, essentially meaning that researchers found that these participants had higher brain activity via EEG. However, the researchers of this study also made it clear that limitations of this study included the fact that only a single ChatGPT model was used (and so participants had no option to choose their own preferred models), there was no division between different tasks, such as “idea generation, writing, and so on, which is often done in prior work”, while they also recommended further study on the impacts of generative AI tools on memory retention, creativity, and writing fluency.   
Then, in November 2025, [researchers at Swansea University](https://www.swansea.ac.uk/press-office/news-events/news/2025/11/can-ai-make-us-more-creative-new-study-reveals-surprising-benefits-of-human-ai-collaboration.php) found that when used properly, generative AI tools could, in fact, enhance humans’ creative thinking. In this study, subjects were asked “to design a simple car to travel as far as possible, within a fixed length of time, on a selection of four different courses”, with each design being tested on a simulation, and the software to create these designs was marketed as a publicly available game on [itch.io](http://itch.io) called “The Genetic Car Designer”, and participants mostly came from Reddit. The software also offered editor and gallery views, with editor views allowing “the human designer to edit existing and create new designs” while gallery views allowed users to choose from static images to select a specification for their design. Gallery views were implemented similarly to GUIComp, which is “Designed to be a companion for GUI design,” specifically the “recommendation panel, which is a subset of a large corpus of screenshots of existing GUIs presented as a gallery.” Some of the recommendations in the study could also “keep” some of the GUIs as templates, deemed by researchers to be “useful for inspiration at the beginning of the design process but… less useful later on.” They found that 50% of participants were considered passive and did not offer any suggestions or inputs to the software’s underlying algorithm, while 36% offered feedback by suggesting designs created in the editor, and 14% offered feedback in both editor and gallery views. Researchers found that participants who actively collaborated with the software used by participants “were more cognitively engaged in the task,” while passive participants “were the least cognitively engaged with the task.” The results were interesting. While there was “no statistical difference between the number of dimensions selected by those who interacted with only the editor and those who interacted with at least one gallery view”, more complex design problems were chosen by passive participants. However, “Participants who viewed the galleries were more cognitively engaged in the task than those who did not.” Furthermore, participants who viewed or interacted with the galleries engaged in the session longer than those who only interacted with the editor, while those who had no interactions at all spent the least time per session. These, then, appeared to correlate with the level of mean improvement across these groups, with those who interacted with at least one gallery achieving a mean improvement of around 420%, compared to a mean improvement of around 124% among passive participants, around 243% for those who only interacted with the editor, and around 373% for those who viewed at leasat one gallery. Suffice it to say, this study challenged many assumptions about what AI should do. For instance, spending less time with a task actually yielded worse results compared to spending more time, with researchers explaining that “Gallery-based human-AI collaborative tools should not be seen as tools to save time and money… but instead tools which lead to better outcomes by enhancing human creativity.” At the same time, simply viewing gallery options seemed to lead to higher levels of creative engagement. Finally, researchers underscored “the need to build transparent and intuitive interfaces that encourages the human designer to engage with the AI.”   
With these principles in mind, what I envision for Creatrweb is to help users of generative AI tools increase their engagement and the level of meaningful creative collaboration with these tools. For instance, [AGENTS.md](http://AGENTS.md) serves as the main orchestration layer for the agent to follow, using specified tools to guide the agent in implementing the Socratic method and proposing gallery options to users for important decisions involved in vibe coding a personal website. It is evident that for any creative project to succeed, humans must do the cognitive work necessary to set the project up for success, whether that means writing an essay themselves or adding sufficient details to [DESIGN.md](http://DESIGN.md) and other documentation in this framework. However, generative AI tools can meaningfully be involved in executing a human’s vision, even if they have never coded or designed before.

## Who This Is For

So, this framework presents an opportunity to help anyone who wants to limit the variance possible with LLMs and hone agentic generative AI coding tools to fulfill their vision, while encouraging their own human creativity and empowering their minds by learning new skills. Whether you're a beginner, intermediate, or advanced developer, engineer, or generative AI consumer, this framework will hopefully encourage you to exercise your creative prowess as you utilize generative AI coding tools. In turn, it should be easier for you to fulfill your creative vision, so long as you approach it meaningfully, build a feature at a time, and refine accordingly.
